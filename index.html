Why Donate Blood?

Blood donation is a simple, safe way to help save lives. Your single donation can help save up to three lives.

The Impact of Your Donation

Every two seconds, someone in the world needs blood. Blood donations are essential for surgeries, cancer treatment, chronic illnesses, and traumatic injuries. Despite medical advances, there is no substitute for human blood.

The Donation Process

Donating blood is simple and takes less than an hour from start to finish.

1

Registration

Complete a donor registration form with your contact information and medical history.

2

Health Check

A quick health screening including blood pressure, pulse, and hemoglobin level check.

3

Donation

Actual blood donation takes about 8-10 minutes. You'll donate about a pint of blood.

4

Refreshment

Enjoy snacks and drinks while you rest for 10-15 minutes before resuming your day.

5

Testing

Your blood is tested for infectious diseases and typed before being sent to hospitals.

6

Delivery

Your blood is delivered to hospitals where it's needed most to save lives.

Frequently Asked Questions

Find answers to common questions about blood donation.

You can donate whole blood every 56 days (8 weeks). Platelet donors can donate more frequently - up to 24 times per year. This allows your body time to replenish the blood you donated.

You may feel a slight pinch when the needle is inserted, but most donors report feeling no discomfort during the donation process. The actual blood donation typically takes about 8-10 minutes.

The entire process - from registration to refreshments - takes about an hour. The actual blood donation only takes 8-10 minutes. First-time donors should plan for about 1.5 hours.

Get a good night's sleep, eat a healthy meal before donating, drink plenty of fluids (avoid caffeine), and bring your ID. Avoid fatty foods as they can affect blood tests.

Your blood is separated into components (red cells, plasma, platelets) and tested for safety. It's then distributed to hospitals for trauma patients, surgeries, cancer treatments, chronic illnesses, and more.
